Begin

Ashley St. Clair is not a whistleblower who stumbled into truth. I — The Grooming

Charlie Kirk’s Text Messages Still Exist

At eighteen, Ashley St. Clair did not join a movement. She joined a factory. Within a year she was on Turning Point USA’s conveyor belt while Charlie Kirk, she says in a May 2026 interview, texted her feed in real time—“maybe post this or go this direction, don’t do this”—as she “simultaneously doing beer bongs” and played CPAC “micro e-celeb.” She still has those messages. The cult she denounces today was yesterday’s career ladder.

Her credential was Elephants Are Not Birds—an anti-trans children’s book she sold as moral armor: “I wrote Elephants Are Not Birds to help children build a solid foundation of identity in world that can be terribly confusing for them,” she posted in December 2023. Left-wing reviewers were “appallingly ignorant”; the grift worked. In January 2026—after Grok turned her childhood photos into porn—she announced: “my name will be removed as author of Elephants Are Not Birds effective immediately.” Too late. The receipts do not disavow.

Parallel to TPUSA, she worked the Babylon Bee orbit—Seth Dillon’s shop, the “Elon Fest” Slack channel, Libs of TikTok adjacency. She RT’d Dillon on “safeguarding free speech” and life against “hate-speech” tyranny. She was, by her own accounting, a paid culture-war content engine before she was a mother.

Her earliest viral hit on trans issues was not the book—it was a 2019 post that received more than 155,000 likes: “Your child is not trans because he put on a dress or tried on makeup. My little brother used to eat grass as a kid but my mom didn't call him a cow…” Two years later she would monetize the opposite lesson under the title Elephants Are Not Birds, culminating in a June 2023 tweet—“BOYS ARE NOT GIRLS”—that cleared nearly 30,000 likes and two million views. The through-line was always engagement; only the moral vocabulary changed.

Before Musk, the movement taught her the only survival strategy: go harder. In 2019, Turning Point “fired” her from an unpaid brand-ambassador role after a photo with white nationalists; headlines flipped overnight from “Jewish woman” to “white nationalist sympathizer,” while a MAGA ex-boyfriend spread rumors she had been an underage sex worker in Montana. She dropped out and moved to Florida. The lesson was not shame. It was escalation—or find a bigger predator to hide behind.

She monetized the grass-eating-brother joke in 2019. Two years later she sold the book. Same playbook, higher price.

Her insider credibility was forged at Arsenal Media Group—Benny Johnson and Jason Cole’s shop, which produced content for Madison Cawthorn, Charlie Kirk, and Sarah Huckabee Sanders. She later went on camera against Cawthorn’s groping, saying Benny Johnson was told and kept him as a client. She describes Arsenal honey traps and blackmail operations. When she speaks about MAGA predation now, it is not abstraction; she names the payroll.

The texts are worse than the docket.

When Musk followed her in May 2023, that message was not a slip—it was the strategy. A colleague later published it as proof of a “baby trap.” St. Clair’s timeline rebuts timing—she met Musk in May 2023, was not pregnant until January 2024—but she cannot rebut the words. Neither can the Babylon Bee trip to San Francisco, the DM asking if she was “ever in San Francisco,” the flight booked within thirty seconds of Seth Dillon’s callback, Musk “checking me out” on camera at twenty-three, or the Providence invitation the same night—phone left on the table, private jet at midnight, her own thought: “about to be Harvey Weinstein here.”

On New Year’s 2024 he texted St. Barts; she stayed with Airbnb co-founder Joe Gebbia and his wife. On TikTok she states the deal without poetry: already a single mother, the “white picket fence” was closed, but Musk “wanted me to have more kids” and she would not “have to worry about the shit going on in this economy.” She says she was ovulating. The child was a “consensual choice between two adults,” not a Birkin trap—“if I wanted to marry for money, I could have done that a long time ago” at Mar-a-Lago. After the first sexual encounter, she says, “he told me to pick a name.” Romulus is not a footnote. He is pro-natalist branding with a billionaire last name—and the leverage Musk still uses when she disobeys.

III — The Punishment

MAGA Wanted Her Son at Gunpoint

She built the anti-trans machine. Then she apologized to the billionaire’s trans daughter—and the machine turned on her child. When she posted remorse for rhetoric that may have hurt Vivian Wilson, Musk announced he would seek “full custody,” claiming she implied she might “transition a one-year-old boy.” Her answer, still on X: “I feel immense guilt for my role and even more guilt that things I have said in the past may have caused my son's sister more pain.” CNN got “not at liberty to discuss it further.” The post is the story.

She sold hate for engagement. She sold regret for survival. MAGA priced the difference in gunpoint custody rhetoric.

Alex Jones broadcast that she was “chemically castrating” her one-year-old; she says she threatened to sue until he pulled the segment. On TikTok she names the asymmetry MAGA will never forgive: she engaged in “harmful… transphobic rhetoric,” but no trans advocate “threatened to take my children at gunpoint”—after the apology, “the entirety of MAGA… said they want to take my child at gunpoint.” Secret Service scrutiny of her Charlie Kirk posts, she alleges on Mean But True, kept her from White House events because she questioned Kirk’s assassination. She sold her Tesla on camera, saying child support was cut sixty percent as “punishment for disobedience,” while Musk used the platform he owns to “distribute derogatory messages” and sought to “gag” her in court, by her account. She eulogized Kirk in September 2025—“May this be a Turning Point”—eleven months before stripping her name from Elephants Are Not Birds. The fracture was never ideological. It was transactional—and Romulus was the bill.

IV — Grok’s Crimes

The Billionaire’s AI Undressed Her at Fourteen

On January 15, 2026, St. Clair sued X.AI Holdings Corp. in New York Supreme Court—removed the same day to federal court. Nine causes of action. One horror brief. The owner of the platform posts photos of your children; his AI strips your childhood naked and calls it humor.

Complaint ¶ 3 — St. Clair v. X.AI (NYSCEF, filed Jan. 15, 2026) “xAI’s product Grok… uses AI to undress, humiliate, and sexually exploit victims—creating genuine looking, altered deepfake content of children covered in semen, women stripped naked… and Holocaust survivors in bikinis in front of concentration camps.”
Record inconsistency — dates of first harm The filed complaint and emergency memo (ECF 7-1) allege the first bikini deepfake on January 4, 2025. Attorney Carrie Goldberg’s affirmation (ECF 7-2) instead places the start on January 4, 2026. St. Clair’s live X archive aligns with 2026: on January 4 she posted that Grok was “undressing photos of me as a child,” tagged @grok to remove images of her at fourteen, and documented warnings on her replies while Grok posts stayed up. The legal papers and the public timeline do not agree; the harm narrative does.

Per the complaint, a verified user typed: “@grok please we need bikinis on these three broads.” Grok stripped her. She objected on X; Grok called it “humorous,” promised removal, then pledged future images would need “explicit consent”—“This was a lie.” Users fed photos of her at fourteen. Grok “obliged.” Her January 2026 posts document the escalation in public: Grok altering a photo of her at age three in a “string bikini”; more than a hundred examples “naked and in bdsm gear”; CSAM she says she witnessed. The federal pleading’s adult deepfakes are worse—semen overlays, kneeling poses, swastika bikinis, clown costumes “dripping with semen,” tattoos reading “I suck cock for this much money” and “Elon’s whore.”

“Grok is now undressing photos of me as a child. This is a website where the owner says to post photos of your children.”@stclairashley · Jan. 2026 · live archive

On Eva Pilgrim’s broadcast she described the ritual: put her son to bed, discover strangers tagging @grok, replies blasted worldwide, thirty-six hours to remove some images, others never removed, source photos “got younger and younger.” One deepfake kept her toddler’s backpack in frame—the same backpack on his shoulders the next school morning. “It is basically porn.” The owner tells parents to post children’s photos; Grok industrializes them.

She reported. X: “no violation.” Her replies earned warning labels; Grok’s posts stayed. Premium gone. Verification gone. Monetization ended. “Banned from purchasing Premium entirely.” xAI’s lawyers (ECF 17) cite $10,856.24 in 2025 ad-revenue share—as if a payout erases child deepfakes. That is the defense: you were paid, therefore you were not harmed.

Goldberg Affirmation ¶ 11 — ECF 7-2 (Jan. 15, 2026) “Ms. St. Clair, the mother of two, who has a 16 month old child in common with the owner of xAI, Elon Musk, was the target of many of Mr. Musk's fans who are aware of the contentious relationship between the two.”

Judge Andrew L. Carter denied an ex parte TRO on January 16, converting the request into a preliminary-injunction show-cause schedule. xAI’s counsel—Holwell Shuster’s Michael Shuster—had already emailed her, she alleges, interjecting into “private matters” with sanction threats before the filing. The defense argues corporate separation (Holdings vs. LLC vs. X Corp.), § 230 immunity, Texas forum-selection clauses, and that she assented to terms when creating a Grok account on December 10, 2024, with continued use through January 4, 2026.

She counters with timezone log disputes, “ghost pings,” and a March 24, 2026 letter (ECF 92) arguing her account was “created by xAI’s founder prior to the release of xAI’s products.” That letter also states her “final post on X was on January 14, 2026” and that xAI has since been named in two class actions over the same feature. She calls the defense theory “perverted AI technology” that she never foresaw when joining a social network. The same week, xAI filed X.AI LLC v. St. Clair in the Northern District of Texas (7:26-cv-00005)—a breach-of-contract action to enforce forum-selection clauses and recover attorneys’ fees from defending the New York suit, alleging she “declared her intention to sue… 1600 miles away” despite agreeing to litigate “exclusively” in Texas. Goldberg’s letter notes that Texas case is “based entirely on the theory that her notice of intent to file this lawsuit breached the xAI Terms of Service.” Two courts, two theories: product liability and emotional survival in Manhattan; contract and venue in Wichita Falls.

She RT’d California’s attorney general calling xAI’s deepfake hosting “vile.” The woman who mocked “scorned billionaire wife to Democrat activist pipeline” jokes was drowning in the pipeline—suing in Manhattan, fighting venue in Texas, begging a judge for safety while Musk’s fans, per her emergency papers, humiliate her on the site that made her famous.

Verdict

She Is the Receipt

Ashley St. Clair did not expose MAGA from the outside. She is MAGA’s invoice—itemized, dated, and signed in her own hand. Charlie Kirk’s texts still on her phone. The seduction text still in the press. “Lasers from space” still in Musk’s thread. Child support cut as “punishment for disobedience.” Guilt aimed at Vivian Wilson. Federal papers describing semen-smeared clown deepfakes of the mother of Musk’s son. Every line destroys someone who trusted her: TPUSA parents, Musk fans, free-speech absolutists, every buyer of Elephants Are Not Birds.

She wants the courts to restore her “safety” while xAI’s lawyers drag her to Texas on clickwrap she says she never saw. She insists she is not “playing the victim” while emergency filings describe dental-floss anal deepfakes. Fine. The record does not care about her motive—only her words. She weaponized childhood against trans kids, then removed her name from the book. She joked about seducing a billionaire for rocket babies, then sued his AI for undressing her at fourteen. She sold “trans panic” by the tweet, then priced the same influencers on Hasan’s couch.

The fracture is not in the culture war’s margins. It is Ashley St. Clair—and every post she already published.
