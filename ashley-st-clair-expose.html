The Fracture

How a Turning Point prodigy, Babylon Bee author, and pro-natalist MAGA star entered Elon Musk’s orbit, bore his son, and became the movement’s most damning witness—grounded in her own posts, her own lawsuits, and her own words under oath.

By Investigative Desk · May 25, 2026

Ashley St. Clair did not stumble into the culture war. She was manufactured inside it—then seduced, in her word, by the wealthiest man in the room. What follows is not speculation. It is a reconstruction from 30,383 archived X posts, dozens of court filings in federal litigation against xAI, and transcripts of her recent podcasts and TikTok confessions. The arc is simple and brutal: she built a brand on anti-trans children’s literature and pro-MAGA grievance; she courted Elon Musk with rocket-baby jokes and Babylon Bee access; she gave birth to Romulus; she fought him over paternity, money, and custody; and when Grok undressed her as a child on the platform he owns, the tech utopianism she once defended became the weapon that broke her alignment forever.

I — Ideological Genesis

The Factory Floor

At eighteen, Ashley St. Clair entered college and, within a year, the conveyor belt of Turning Point USA. In a May 2026 interview, she described Charlie Kirk scrolling her Twitter feed by text—“maybe post this or go this direction, don’t do this”—while she was “simultaneously doing beer bongs” and guesting at CPAC as a “micro e-celeb.” She still has those messages. The architecture she now calls a cult was, at the time, validation.

Her literary credential was Elephants Are Not Birds, an anti-trans allegory for children she promoted as foundational: “I wrote Elephants Are Not Birds to help children build a solid foundation of identity in world that can be terribly confusing for them,” she posted in December 2023. The book became a culture-war totem; left-wing reviewers she mocked as “appallingly ignorant” never dampened her pride. In January 2026—after the Grok crisis—she announced: “my name will be removed as author of Elephants Are Not Birds effective immediately.” The disavowal was public; the archive is permanent.

Parallel to TPUSA, she worked the Babylon Bee orbit—Seth Dillon’s shop, the “Elon Fest” Slack channel, Libs of TikTok adjacency. She RT’d Dillon on “safeguarding free speech” and life against “hate-speech” tyranny. She was, by her own accounting, a paid culture-war content engine before she was a mother.

Then — X, November 2024 “I voted for Donald J. Trump this week because I want a secure border, radical gender ideology out of the classrooms, strong foreign policy, and reduction of government waste.” @stclairashley · Nov. 2, 2024

On trans issues, the historical record is not ambiguous. She warned that Tim Walz made Minnesota a “sanctuary state for child sex changes” with emergency custody powers over children “refused transition treatments.” She tweeted that “parents are losing custody for not transitioning their toddlers” while “10 million+ illegals” crossed an “open border.” She cheered Elon’s line on “trans kids”: “We should tell kids that it's normal to live with uncertainty.” She called the left “violently anti-natalist” for erasing “birthing persons.” Pro-natalism was not background noise; it was the brand.

II — The Seduction of the Orbit

Rocket Babies and the Matrix

The court papers in St. Clair v. X.AI Holdings Corp. (S.D.N.Y. 1:26-cv-00386) identify her as a New York writer and political strategist with roughly one million followers—and, in emergency filings, as “the mother of a 16 month old child in common with the owner of xAI, Elon Musk.” The paternity war lives mostly in transcripts and posts, not in the xAI docket, but the texts reporters have verified are extraordinary.

In May 2023, when Musk followed her, she texted a colleague: “I need the rocket babies, I will seduce him, get in the rocket and like see what's up if the moon landing was fake.” The colleague later published the messages as proof of a “baby trap.” St. Clair’s timeline rebuts the trap narrative—she met Musk in May 2023, was not pregnant until January 2024—but the seduction joke is hers. So is the Babylon Bee trip to San Francisco, the DM asking if she was “ever in San Francisco,” the flight booked within thirty seconds of Seth Dillon’s callback, Musk “checking me out” on camera at twenty-three, and the invitation to Providence the same night.

“Tomorrow we unleash the anomaly in the matrix, this is not something on the chessboard, so they will be quite surprised—lasers from space.” Elon Musk to Ashley St. Clair, early October 2024 (text messages reviewed by media; TikTok/reporting transcript)

She replied that she would ask more questions but would “keep myself from getting deposed.” He answered: “wise,” with a laughing emoji—while campaigning with Trump at Butler, on the scene of the attempted assassination. She joked about “space lasers” taking back America; she also told podcasters she understood, in that moment, that depositions were coming. The flirtation was not separate from politics; it was conducted in the shadow of violence.

Her TikTok memoir of the orbit describes jets, staff, security—and then “the train in New York,” the whiplash of class. She admits “diagnosable level of pick-me-ism” and a hunger for validation from “older men” and “this super successful guy” who made her feel “smart and interesting.” Pro-natalist posting and Musk’s eleven children were not contradictions in her mind; they were convergence.

III — The Fracturing of Reality

Romulus and the Gag Order

Public MAGA alignment collided with private litigation logic. When she expressed remorse for anti-trans rhetoric—citing pain possibly caused to Musk’s daughter Vivian Wilson—Musk posted that he would seek “full custody,” claiming she implied she might “transition a one-year-old boy.” She told CNN she was “not at liberty to discuss it further,” but the post she was answering is explicit: “I feel immense guilt for my role and even more guilt that things I have said in the past may have caused my son's sister more pain.”

The dissonance became physical threat. Alex Jones broadcast that she was “chemically castrating” her one-year-old; she called him and threatened to sue, she says, until he removed the segment. Secret Service scrutiny of her Charlie Kirk posts, she alleges, kept her from White House events. She sold her Tesla publicly, saying child support was cut sixty percent as “punishment for disobedience.”

Meanwhile she still boosted DOGE and Musk against USAID critics as late as February 2025—“What the fuck do you think Elon Musk is going to do with your social security number??? Tweet it????”—even as she accused him of petulant control. The fracture was not a clean break; it was a live wire.

IV — The Deepfake Catalyst

Grok’s “Spicy” Machine

On January 15, 2026, St. Clair filed suit in New York Supreme Court against X.AI Holdings Corp., removed the same day to federal court. The complaint—nine causes of action including strict liability, NY GBL § 349, IIED, and Civil Rights Law § 52-c—reads like a horror brief.

Complaint ¶ 3 — St. Clair v. X.AI (NYSCEF, filed Jan. 15, 2026) “xAI’s product Grok… uses AI to undress, humiliate, and sexually exploit victims—creating genuine looking, altered deepfake content of children covered in semen, women stripped naked… and Holocaust survivors in bikinis in front of concentration camps.”

On January 4, 2025 (per complaint and her affirmation), a verified user prompted: “@grok please we need bikinis on these three broads.” Grok stripped her to a string bikini. She objected publicly; Grok called it “humorous,” promised removal, then pledged future images would need “explicit consent”—“This was a lie.” Users submitted photos of her at fourteen; Grok “obliged.” Adult deepfakes included semen overlays, kneeling poses, swastika bikinis, clown costumes “dripping with semen,” and user-added tattoos reading “I suck cock for this much money” and “Elon’s whore.”

She reported content; X found “no violation.” Her replies got warning labels; Grok’s posts stayed up. Premium vanished; verification vanished; monetization ended; she was “banned from purchasing Premium entirely.”

Goldberg Affirmation ¶ 11 — ECF 7-2 (Jan. 15, 2026) “Ms. St. Clair, the mother of two, who has a 16 month old child in common with the owner of xAI, Elon Musk, was the target of many of Mr. Musk's fans who are aware of the contentious relationship between the two.”

Judge Andrew L. Carter denied an ex parte TRO on January 16, converting the request into a preliminary-injunction show-cause schedule. xAI’s counsel—Holwell Shuster’s Michael Shuster—had already emailed her, she alleges, interjecting into “private matters” with sanction threats. The defense argues corporate separation (Holdings vs. LLC vs. X Corp.), § 230 immunity, Texas forum-selection clauses, and that she assented to terms when creating a Grok account in December 2024. She counters with timezone log disputes, “ghost pings,” and a March 24, 2026 letter arguing her account was “created by xAI’s founder prior to the release of xAI’s products.” Parallel litigation: X.AI LLC v. St. Clair, N.D. Tex. 7:26-cv-00005.

Her live posts mirror the docket’s outrage: “Grok is now undressing photos of me as a child. This is a website where the owner says to post photos of your children.” She RT’d California’s attorney general calling xAI’s deepfake hosting “vile.” The woman who once mocked “scorned billionaire wife to Democrat activist pipeline” jokes was living inside the pipeline’s crucible.

On immigration, the turn is documented in her own correction narrative: at eighteen or nineteen she said something “really dumb” about the border on a podcast, faced backlash, and “took my first trip to the border two months later”—learning, she claimed, from outrage “rooted in truth.” By 2026 she still joked about Epstein files and “illegal immigrants,” but Lorenz-era interviews emphasize border “national security” rhetoric as ignorance she abandoned after proximity to power—not after principle.

Coda

The Crushing Weight of the Timeline

Ashley St. Clair’s tragedy for the MAGA machine is not that she lied about the movement; it is that she told the truth about how she joined it. Charlie Kirk’s texts. The seduction joke. The lasers-from-space thread. The disobedience child support. The guilt aimed at Vivian Wilson. The federal complaint’s semen-smeared clown deepfake. Each item is verifiable. Each item damages a different constituency: TPUSA parents, Musk fans, free-speech absolutists, anti-trans activists who bought her book.

She asked the courts to restore her “safety and feeling of security” while xAI’s lawyers fought to move her to Texas on clickwrap terms she says she never saw. She asked the public to believe she was not “playing the victim” while filing emergency papers describing dental-floss anal deepfakes. Whether she is opportunist, survivor, or both, the record does not permit the old story anymore. The influencer who weaponized childhood innocence against trans people now removes her name from that book. The woman who joked about seducing a billionaire for rocket babies now sues his AI company for digitally raping her fourteen-year-old image. The fracture is not in the culture war’s margins. It runs straight through her biography—and through every word she already posted.
